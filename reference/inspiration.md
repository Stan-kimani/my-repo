# Inspiration & competitors

## Sites I admire
- **Robert Half** (roberthalf.com) — clean enterprise-grade layout, strong authority
  signals, reassuring tone that makes the service feel established rather than scrappy.
- **Randstad Canada** (randstad.ca) — good balance of employer/candidate messaging, clear
  service categories, a modern staffing flow that feels scalable and familiar.

## Research roundups (pattern sources, not sites to copy)
- **Manatal — recruitment site roundup** — pattern recognition on what recruitment sites
  do well, especially CTA clarity and candidate-facing trust cues.
- **Colorlib — recruitment examples** — layout and visual-direction ideas across styles,
  premium to practical.
- **Colorlib — VA website examples** — premium assistant-style positioning: how sites make
  support feel polished, human, and low-friction.

## Competitors
- **Robert Half** — does well: premium corporate credibility, polished brand language,
  clear specialization. | does badly: broad and corporate; lacks the intimate "boutique"
  feel a smaller premium operator can own.
- **Randstad** — does well: high trust, broad coverage, strong staffing recognition. |
  does badly: reads as a large institutional agency, not a tailored operator-matching service.
- **Talent.com** — does well: strong search utility, scale orientation. | does badly:
  transactional and marketplace-like — misaligned with a curated, high-touch model.
- **Paradox** — does well: modern automation-forward hiring, strong digital sophistication.
  | does badly: feels more tech platform than human service, reducing warmth for premium clients.

## Anti-patterns to avoid
- Task-list-heavy copy that reads like an internal ops manual instead of a client-facing promise.
- Stock-photo overload — generic smiling teams, laptop-at-café shots — that weakens credibility.
- Hidden pricing or vague "contact us for details" CTAs that create friction too early.
- Too many competing CTAs on one page, diluting the trust narrative. (RemoteKind ships ONE CTA.)
- Buzzwords — "world-class," "revolutionary," "seamless" — without proof.
- Overly long forms before trust is established.
- Generic recruitment language that could belong to any agency.

## Design direction (synthesis — this is what to build toward)
Every competitor above is a large, institutional platform, and each fails the same way:
corporate, broad, transactional, more tech than human. That's the opening.

**Borrow their trust signals; reject their impersonal scale.**
- Take: the polished authority, clear specialization, and reassuring "established" tone
  (Robert Half's credibility, Randstad's clarity).
- Leave: the institutional coldness, the marketplace feel, the automation-over-humans vibe.
- Own what they can't: a boutique, curated, high-touch register — RemoteKind feels like a
  hand-selected match from a real person, not a search result from an agency.

Practical implications for the build:
- Warm, specific, human copy over generic agency language (supports the serif-headline
  typography direction in brand.md).
- Real proof (the Lynet testimonial, the vetting bar) over buzzwords.
- One confident CTA, no friction, no early forms — book a call.
- Restrained visuals; avoid the stock-photo trap. Founder photo > generic team shots.
